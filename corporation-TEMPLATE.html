    Nature of Corporations

    A corporation is an artificial being created by operation of law, endowed with the right of succession and vested with the powers, attributes, and properties expressly authorized by law or necessarily incidental to its existence.

    The Juridical Personality

    The defining concept of a corporation is its status as a "juridical person." This means the law grants it a personality separate and distinct from the people who own it. While a human is a "natural person" with biological life, the corporation is an "artificial being" that exists only on paper and in the eyes of the law.

    Its agency is exercised indirectly through human agents—directors, officers, employees, and agents—who make decisions and perform acts in the corporation’s name. The corporation’s rights and duties are primarily legal and instrumental: it may own property, enter contracts, sue and be sued, and incur obligations, but these capacities are conferred and limited by statute and charter.

    Unlike human beings, who are concrete biological substances with direct causal powers, moral consciousness, and personal responsibility, a corporation’s personhood is institutional and dependent on social and legal practices; it can be held liable and regulated, yet its moral agency is mediated through the people who run it. Because corporate personhood is a legal fiction, courts may protect it to promote commerce and stability, but they may also disregard or pierce the corporate veil when the fiction is abused, fraudulent, or used to defeat public policy or creditors’ rights.
