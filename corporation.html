    Introduction to the Topic: Corporation

    A comprehensive guide to understanding corporate structures.

    Day 1: Legal Attributes and Classifications of Corporations


    • To remember the specific statutes, such as the Revised Corporation Code, that provide the legal framework for corporate existence.
    • To understand the nature of a corporation as a legal fiction and the characteristics that separate it from its owners.
    • To analyze the various classifications of corporations to determine their regulatory requirements and legal standing.

    Learning Objectives

    In this initial module, we lay the foundation for corporate law by exploring the "life" of a corporation from a legal perspective. The goal is not just to memorize definitions, but to build a framework for corporate analysis.

    Students will be expected to remember the governing laws (R.A. 11232 and others) and understand why the law treats a corporation as an "artificial being" distinct from human stockholders. Furthermore, we will analyze how the law categorizes these entities‚Äîwhether by the number of people who formed them, their country of origin, or the perfection of their legal registration.

    Nature of Corporations

    A corporation is an artificial being created by operation of law, endowed with the right of succession and vested with the powers, attributes, and properties expressly authorized by law or necessarily incidental to its existence.

    The Juridical Personality

    The defining concept of a corporation is its status as a "juridical person." This means the law grants it a personality separate and distinct from the people who own it. While a human is a "natural person" with biological life, the corporation is an "artificial being" that exists only on paper and in the eyes of the law.

    This separation creates the "Corporate Veil." Under normal circumstances, the debts of the corporation are not the debts of the owners. However, if this legal fiction is used to commit fraud or shield illegal acts, the courts can "pierce the veil," holding the individuals personally liable for the corporation's actions.

    Governing Laws

    Corporations are primarily governed by the Revised Corporation Code of the Philippines (R.A. 11232).

    • Revised Corporation Code (R.A. 11232) ‚Äî Governance and formation.
    • Securities Regulation Code (R.A. 8799) ‚Äî SEC oversight.
    • Foreign Investments Act (R.A. 7042) ‚Äî Foreign equity rules.
    • Special Laws ‚Äî Banking and Insurance codes.

    The Legal Framework

    In the Philippines, the "Bible" of corporate law is Republic Act No. 11232, also known as the Revised Corporation Code. It modernized our laws by allowing for One-Person Corporations and perpetual existence. However, this is not the only law involved.

    Publicly listed companies must also follow the Securities Regulation Code, while foreign-owned entities must comply with the Foreign Investments Act. Furthermore, the Philippine Constitution restricts certain industries (like land ownership or utilities) to corporations that are at least 60% Filipino-owned, a concept known as "Nationalized Industries."

    Characteristics of a Corporation

    • Separate Legal Personality: Distinct from its shareholders.
    • Limited Liability: Owners are only liable up to their investment.
    • Free Transferability: Shares can be sold without dissolving the company.
    • Centralized Management: Governed by a Board of Directors.

    The Four Pillars of Corporate Form

    Why do entrepreneurs choose the corporate form over a partnership? It comes down to four essential pillars. Separate Personality means the corporation can own its own car or bank account. Limited Liability protects the owner's personal house and savings from business creditors.

    Free Transferability allows an investor to leave the business simply by selling their stock, without needing the permission of other owners. Finally, Centralized Management ensures efficiency; instead of every owner trying to run the store, they elect a Board of Directors to make the big decisions.

    Classification by Number of Incorporators

    • Corporation Sole: A single religious head managing church property.
    • One Person Corporation (OPC): A single stockholder (natural person, trust, or estate).
    • Ordinary Corporation: Two or more incorporators.

    Who can form a Corporation?

    Under the new law, the requirements for who can start a corporation have become very flexible. The traditional Ordinary Corporation requires a group of people (incorporators) to sign the founding documents. Historically, you needed at least five people, but the modern code has relaxed these numbers.

    The One Person Corporation (OPC) is a revolutionary change in Philippine law. It allows a single individual to enjoy the benefits of limited liability without finding partners. The Corporation Sole remains a special religious category, allowing a Bishop or Priest to hold property in the name of the church rather than in their own personal name.

    Special Corporations

    • Educational Corporations: Governing schools and universities.
    • Religious Corporations: Governing religious groups or sects.

    Non-Commercial Entities

    Not all corporations are built to sell products. Special Corporations are created for purposes that the State deems vital for the public good. Educational Corporations are strictly regulated by the Department of Education or CHED to ensure academic standards are met.

    Religious Corporations provide a way for faith-based groups to have a legal identity. By incorporating, a church can legally sign a lease for a building or open a bank account to manage donations, ensuring the organization outlasts its current leaders.

    Classification by Nationality

    • Domestic Corporation: Incorporated under Philippine laws.
    • Foreign Corporation: Formed under the laws of another country.

    Domestic vs. Foreign

    A corporation's "citizenship" is determined by where it was born (incorporated). A Domestic Corporation is a child of the Philippine SEC and follows all local rules. A Foreign Corporation is created in a different country but may want to do business here.

    To operate legally in the Philippines, a foreign corporation must obtain a "License to do Business" from the SEC. Without this license, they cannot use our court system to sue someone, although they can still be sued by Filipinos. This ensures that foreign entities are accountable to our local laws while they operate within our borders.

    Other Classifications

    • Public vs. Private: Government entities vs. privately owned aims.
    • De Jure vs. De Facto: Perfect legal compliance vs. flawed but functional existence.

    Legal Perfection and Purpose

    When looking at purpose, Public Corporations are those like cities or municipalities created to govern people. Private Corporations are formed by individuals for their own benefit, whether for profit (Stock) or charity (Non-stock).

    From a technical legal standpoint, we look at De Jure and De Facto status. A De Jure corporation has done everything perfectly according to the SEC. A De Facto corporation has a "defect"‚Äîmaybe a missing signature or a typo in the filings‚Äîbut because they acted in good faith, the law recognizes them as a corporation for most business purposes to protect the people who deal with them.

    Checkpoint Question 1: Governing Laws

    Which specific statute establishes the Foreign Investment Negative List and prescribes rules for foreign enterprises doing business in the Philippines?

    • A) R.A. 11232 (Revised Corporation Code)
    • B) R.A. 8791 (General Banking Law)
    • C) R.A. 8799 (Securities Regulation Code)
    • D) R.A. 7042 (Foreign Investments Act)
    Correct Answer: D. R.A. 7042 (Foreign Investments Act, as amended)

    Review Challenge: Governing Laws

    It is vital for accountants and managers to know which specific laws apply to different corporate scenarios. While the Revised Corporation Code (A) is the general rulebook, foreign ownership is a specialized area of law.

    The correct answer is D (R.A. 7042). This law maintains the "Negative List," which lists industries where foreigners are prohibited or limited (like mass media or small-scale mining).

    Checkpoint Question 2: Nature of Corporations

    Because corporate personhood is a legal fiction, under what specific circumstance will courts disregard or "pierce the corporate veil"?

    • A) When the corporation fails to generate profit for three consecutive years.
    • B) When the fiction is abused, fraudulent, or used to defeat public policy or creditors' rights.
    • C) When the corporation engages in a joint venture with a foreign entity.
    • D) When all corporate shares are transferred to a single individual.
    Correct Answer: B. When the fiction is abused, fraudulent, or used to defeat public policy or creditors' rights.

    Review Challenge: The Corporate Veil

    The "Corporate Veil" is a legal barrier that protects stockholders from being held liable for the corporation's debts. However, this protection is not absolute. If the court finds that the owners are using the company as a "dummy" or "alter ego" to commit fraud, they will pierce the veil.

    Option B is correct because legal personhood is a privilege granted by the State to promote business stability, not to shield criminal activity. Options A, C, and D are perfectly legal scenarios and do not warrant losing limited liability protection.

    Checkpoint Question 3: Incorporators

    An entity formed by a single stockholder, who can specifically be a natural person, a trust, or an estate, is legally classified as:

    • A) A Corporation Sole
    • B) A De Facto Corporation
    • C) A One Person Corporation (OPC)
    • D) A Public Corporation
    Correct Answer: C. A One Person Corporation (OPC).

    Review Challenge: Single-Owner Entities

    It is easy to confuse a One Person Corporation (OPC) with a Corporation Sole. The key difference lies in the nature of the owner. A Corporation Sole (A) is strictly for a religious leader (like a Bishop) to manage church properties.

    The correct answer is C. The Revised Corporation Code specifically allows a natural person, a trust, or an estate to be the "single stockholder" of an OPC. This allows individual entrepreneurs to gain corporate protections without needing to include "nominee" partners just to meet a headcount requirement.

    Checkpoint Question 4: Legal Status

    A corporation that acts as a corporation and made a good-faith attempt to organize under valid law, but possesses a specific flaw in its incorporation process, is classified as:

    • A) A De Jure Corporation
    • B) An Ordinary Corporation
    • C) A Domestic Corporation
    • D) A De Facto Corporation
    Correct Answer: D. A De Facto Corporation.

    Review Challenge: Legal Existence

    This question tests your ability to distinguish between "perfect" and "flawed" legal existence. A De Jure Corporation (A) has strictly complied with all legal requirements and is immune to legal attacks regarding its formation.

    The correct answer is D. A De Facto corporation is one that is "good enough" for the time being. As long as there was a valid law and a good-faith effort to comply, the corporation's acts remain valid to protect third parties, even if the government might later challenge its registration due to the flaw.

    Checkpoint Question 5: Nationality

    For a foreign corporation to legally conduct business in the Philippines and be capable of being sued in local courts, what must happen?

    • A) It must reorganize internally to have exactly 15 Filipino incorporators.
    • B) It must obtain a specific SEC license, and its home country must grant reciprocal rights.
    • C) It must surrender its limited liability shield for local operations.
    • D) It must re-classify itself specifically as an Educational Corporation.
    Correct Answer: B. It must obtain a specific SEC license, and its home country laws must allow local citizens reciprocal rights to do business.

    Review Challenge: Foreign Entities

    National sovereignty dictates that a foreign company cannot just walk into the Philippines and start selling without permission. The correct answer is B because the State needs to ensure two things: First, that the foreign company is registered and "sue-able" here (via the SEC License); and second, that Filipinos are allowed to do business in that company's home country (Reciprocity).

    Option A is incorrect because nationality is based on where the entity was organized, not the specific number of local people in it. Option C is wrong because the whole point of a corporation is to keep that limited liability shield regardless of nationality.

    Day 2: Formation, Registration, and Capital Structure


    • To remember the specific steps for SEC registration and the mandatory post-incorporation compliance requirements.
    • To understand the legal significance of the Articles of Incorporation and the specific roles of the founding incorporators.
    • To analyze the financial structure of a new corporation by distinguishing between authorized, subscribed, and paid-up capital stock.

    Learning Objectives

    In this module, we transition from the theory of what a corporation is, to the practical reality of how to build one. The formation of a corporation is a strict legal process governed by the State.

    Students will be expected to remember the exact procedures for registering with the Securities and Exchange Commission and the subsequent agencies like the BIR and LGUs. You must understand the power of the Articles of Incorporation as a binding contract. Finally, we will analyze the initial financial terminology‚Äîauthorized, subscribed, and paid-up capital‚Äîwhich dictates how much money the corporation is legally allowed to raise and how much it actually possesses upon birth.

    SEC Registration Process

    The formal legal existence of a corporation commences only upon the issuance of the Certificate of Incorporation by the SEC.


    • Name Verification: Reserving a distinct and legally permissible corporate name.
    • Document Preparation: Drafting the Articles of Incorporation and By-laws.
    • Online Filing: Submission of signed documents through the SEC online portal.
    • Payment of Fees: Settling filing and legal research fees based on authorized capital.
    • Issuance of Certificate: The official act granting juridical personality to the corporation.

    The Birth of a Corporation

    A corporation does not exist simply because people agree to do business together; it must be formally birthed by the State through the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC).

    The process begins with securing a unique corporate name to prevent public confusion. The founders then draft the necessary documents, primarily the Articles of Incorporation and By-laws, and submit them via the SEC's online portal. The critical moment occurs when the SEC, satisfied with the documents and the payment of filing fees, issues the Certificate of Incorporation. It is exactly at this moment that the corporation gains its separate juridical personality and limited liability protections.

    Articles of Incorporation (AOI)

    The Articles of Incorporation is the basic contract document defining the corporation's charter, powers, and fundamental structure.


    • Corporate Name & Purpose: Identifies the entity and defines the scope of its authorized business activities.
    • Principal Office: Establishes the official legal address within the Philippines.
    • Corporate Term: Grants perpetual existence by default under the Revised Corporation Code.
    • Founders: Lists the names, nationalities, and residences of the incorporators.
    • Capital Structure: Details the authorized capital stock, number of shares, and initial subscriber commitments.

    The Supreme Charter

    The Articles of Incorporation (AOI) is the most important document in a corporation's existence. It acts as a three-way contract: between the State and the corporation, between the corporation and its shareholders, and among the shareholders themselves.

    The AOI dictates what the corporation is legally allowed to do (its purpose). If a corporation engages in business outside of this stated purpose, those acts are considered ultra vires (beyond its powers). The document also locks in the principal office address, which is crucial because it dictates the legal venue where the corporation can be sued or file lawsuits.

    Incorporators & Initial Management

    Incorporators are the signatories to the AOI who bring the corporation into existence, while the Board of Directors manages its operations.

    • Qualifications: Can be natural persons of legal age, partnerships, associations, or other corporations.
    • Number required: Usually 2 to 15 for an ordinary corporation (or just 1 for a One Person Corporation).
    • Shareholding Requirement: Each incorporator of a stock corporation must own or subscribe to at least one (1) share.
    • Initial Directors: The AOI names the interim board responsible for organizational tasks until the first shareholder election.

    Founders and First Managers

    Incorporators are the original founders. Their names and signatures are permanently etched into the Articles of Incorporation. To be an incorporator of a stock corporation, the law strictly requires that you must own or subscribe to at least one share of the capital stock; you cannot found a company you have no stake in.

    Because a newly birthed corporation needs immediate leadership, the AOI also names the Initial Directors. This interim board acts as the caretakers of the company, handling the immediate organizational tasks‚Äîlike opening a bank account and electing the first corporate officers‚Äîuntil the shareholders can hold their first official meeting to vote for a permanent board.

    Understanding Capitalization Terms

    The financial structure of a stock corporation is categorized into authorized, subscribed, and paid-up capital stock.


    • Authorized Capital Stock: The absolute maximum value and number of shares the corporation can issue based on its AOI.
    • Subscribed Capital Stock: The portion of the authorized capital that investors have legally committed to purchase.
    • Paid-up Capital: The actual portion of the subscribed capital that has been paid to the corporate treasury.
    • General Rule: The Revised Corporation Code eliminated minimum subscribed and paid-up requirements for ordinary corporations upon incorporation, subject to special laws.

    The Financial Foundation

    To analyze a corporation's equity, you must understand three distinct layers of capitalization. Authorized Capital Stock is the legal ceiling. It is the absolute maximum number of shares the company is allowed to print and sell, as defined in its Articles of Incorporation.

    [Image illustrating a funnel: Large Authorized Capital pouring into a smaller Subscribed Capital, pouring into an even smaller Paid-up Capital]

    Subscribed Capital Stock represents the legally binding promises made by investors to buy a portion of those authorized shares. Finally, Paid-up Capital is the hard reality‚Äîit represents the actual cash or property that has already been handed over to the corporate treasury by the subscribers. Under the modernized code, the old rules requiring 25% subscription and 25% paid-up capital upon formation have generally been eliminated to encourage business creation.

    Post-Incorporation Requirements

    SEC registration provides legal existence, but a corporation must comply with other government agency requirements before commencing commercial operations.

    • Adoption of By-laws: Must be filed within the prescribed period to govern internal corporate administration.
    • BIR Registration: Securing a Tax Identification Number (TIN), registering books of accounts, and Authority to Print (ATP) invoices.
    • Local Government Units (LGU): Obtaining Barangay Clearance and the Mayor's / Business Permit.
    • Statutory Benefits: Registering as an employer with SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG.
    • Secondary Licenses: Procuring specialized permits from agencies (e.g., BSP, Insurance Commission) if entering regulated industries.

    Beyond the SEC Certificate

    Obtaining the SEC Certificate of Incorporation grants the corporation legal life, but it does not grant the right to immediately open shop and sell goods. A series of secondary compliances must be met.

    Internally, the corporation must adopt its By-laws, which are the internal rules governing how meetings are run and officers are elected. Externally, the corporation must register with the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) to legally issue invoices, and with the Local Government Unit (LGU) for a Mayor's Permit. Finally, to protect future employees, it must register with statutory benefit agencies like SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG.

    Checkpoint Question 6: SEC Registration

    Which specific event officially grants a separate juridical personality to the newly formed corporation?

    • A) The successful online filing of the Articles of Incorporation.
    • B) The reservation of a distinct and legally permissible corporate name.
    • C) The payment of filing and legal research fees based on authorized capital.
    • D) The issuance of the Certificate of Incorporation by the SEC.
    Correct Answer: D. The issuance of the Certificate of Incorporation by the SEC.

    Review: The Birth of the Entity

    The correct answer is D. While drafting documents and paying fees are necessary precursors, the corporation does not legally exist until the State formally recognizes it by issuing the Certificate of Incorporation.

    Checkpoint Question 7: Articles of Incorporation

    According to the Articles of Incorporation, what is the specific legal significance of designating the principal office address?

    • A) It determines the tax rate applied by the Bureau of Internal Revenue.
    • B) It establishes the legal venue for corporate actions and lawsuits.
    • C) It defines the geographic limits where the corporation can sell its products.
    • D) It dictates the nationality of the corporation's founding incorporators.
    Correct Answer: B. It establishes the legal venue for corporate actions and lawsuits.

    Review: The Principal Office

    The correct answer is B. The principal office listed in the AOI is the official residence of the corporation. In legal disputes, this address determines which specific city or provincial court has jurisdiction over the case.

    Checkpoint Question 8: Incorporators

    What is a strict, mandatory requirement for an individual or entity to act as an incorporator of a stock corporation?

    • A) They must own or subscribe to at least one share of the capital stock.
    • B) They must be a Filipino citizen residing in the principal office's municipality.
    • C) They must contribute a minimum paid-up capital of 5,000 pesos.
    • D) They must serve on the initial Board of Directors for a minimum of five years.
    Correct Answer: A. They must own or subscribe to at least one (1) share of the capital stock.

    Review: Incorporator Requirements

    The correct answer is A. To be a founding member of a stock corporation, the law requires that you have some "skin in the game." You cannot form a stock entity without subscribing to at least a single share.

    Checkpoint Question 9: Capitalization

    How does the Revised Corporation Code generally treat the minimum requirements for subscribed and paid-up capital upon incorporation for ordinary corporations?

    • A) It strictly maintains the traditional 25% subscription and 25% paid-up rule.
    • B) It dictates that all subscribed capital must be fully paid-up before the SEC issues a certificate.
    • C) It eliminated minimum subscribed and paid-up requirements, except when mandated by special laws.
    • D) It requires a fixed minimum paid-up capital of 1,000,000 pesos for all new domestic corporations.
    Correct Answer: C. It eliminated minimum subscribed and paid-up requirements, except when mandated by special industry laws.

    Review: Minimum Capital Rules

    The correct answer is C. The Revised Corporation Code removed the old 25%-25% rule to make it easier for entrepreneurs to start businesses. However, special laws governing industries like banking or insurance still impose strict minimum capital requirements.

    Checkpoint Question 10: Post-Incorporation

    Following SEC registration, what specific BIR compliance step must a corporation fulfill to legally issue official receipts to its customers?

    • A) Adopt and submit the corporate By-laws.
    • B) Secure an Authority to Print (ATP) invoices.
    • C) Obtain a Mayor's Business Permit.
    • D) Register as an employer with SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG.
    Correct Answer: B. Secure an Authority to Print (ATP) invoices.

    Review: Post-Registration Compliance

    The correct answer is B. While the SEC handles legal formation, the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) manages taxation. You cannot legally print or issue official sales invoices without first securing an ATP from the BIR.
