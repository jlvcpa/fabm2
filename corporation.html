    • To remember the governing laws, SEC registration steps, and post-incorporation compliance requirements.
    • To understand the legal nature of a corporation, its defining characteristics, and the significance of the Articles of Incorporation.

    Learning Objectives

    In this comprehensive Day 1 module, we lay the foundation for corporate law by exploring the "life" of a corporation from a legal and structural perspective. The goal is to build a framework for corporate analysis.

    Students will be expected to remember the governing laws (R.A. 11232 and others) and the exact procedural steps for SEC registration. You must understand why the law treats a corporation as an "artificial being" and how the Articles of Incorporation act as the supreme contract. Furthermore, we will analyze how the law categorizes these entities and deconstruct their initial financial terminology‚Äîauthorized, subscribed, and paid-up capital.

    Nature of Corporations

    A corporation is an artificial being created by operation of law, endowed with the right of succession and vested with the powers, attributes, and properties expressly authorized by law or necessarily incidental to its existence.

    The Juridical Personality

    The defining concept of a corporation is its status as a "juridical person." This means the law grants it a personality separate and distinct from the people who own it. While a human is a "natural person" with biological life, the corporation is an "artificial being" that exists only on paper and in the eyes of the law.

    Its agency is exercised indirectly through human agents‚Äîdirectors, officers, employees, and agents‚Äîwho make decisions and perform acts in the corporation‚Äôs name. The corporation‚Äôs rights and duties are primarily legal and instrumental: it may own property, enter contracts, sue and be sued, and incur obligations, but these capacities are conferred and limited by statute and charter.

    Unlike human beings, who are concrete biological substances with direct causal powers, moral consciousness, and personal responsibility, a corporation‚Äôs personhood is institutional and dependent on social and legal practices; it can be held liable and regulated, yet its moral agency is mediated through the people who run it. Because corporate personhood is a legal fiction, courts may protect it to promote commerce and stability, but they may also disregard or pierce the corporate veil when the fiction is abused, fraudulent, or used to defeat public policy or creditors‚Äô rights.

    Governing Laws

    Corporations are primarily governed by the Revised Corporation Code of the Philippines (R.A. 11232), which sets out formation, corporate powers, governance, and dissolution.

    • Revised Corporation Code (R.A. 11232) ‚Äî formation, corporate attributes, governance, and dissolution.
    • Securities Regulation Code (R.A. 8799) ‚Äî regulation of securities, public offerings, and SEC oversight.
    • Foreign Investments Act and related statutes ‚Äî rules on foreign equity, restrictions, and incentives.
    • Special laws and sectoral codes ‚Äî e.g., Banking Law, Insurance Code, Cooperative Code, Public Utilities regulations; these govern corporations in regulated industries.
    • Constitutional provisions ‚Äî national patrimony and ownership limitations that affect corporate structure and permissible activities.

    The Legal Framework

    In the Philippines, the primary authority for corporate law is Republic Act No. 11232, also known as the Revised Corporation Code. It modernized our laws by allowing for One-Person Corporations and perpetual existence. However, specialized laws also apply depending on the corporation's activities and ownership.

    Publicly listed companies must follow the Securities Regulation Code, while foreign-owned entities comply with the Foreign Investments Act. Sectoral laws like the General Banking Law impose strict licensing for regulated industries. Furthermore, the Philippine Constitution restricts certain industries, such as land ownership or public utilities, to corporations that meet specific Filipino ownership percentages.

    Characteristics of a Corporation

    • Separate Legal Personality: Distinct from its shareholders and members.
    • Limited Liability: Shareholders are generally only liable up to their investment amount.
    • Free Transferability: Shares can usually be bought or sold without dissolving the corporation.
    • Centralized Management: Governed by a Board of Directors or Trustees.

    The Four Pillars of Corporate Form

    Why do entrepreneurs choose the corporate form over a partnership? It comes down to four essential pillars. Separate Personality means the corporation can own its own assets or bank account. Limited Liability protects the owner's personal house and savings from business creditors.

    Free Transferability allows an investor to leave the business simply by selling their stock, without needing the permission of other owners. Finally, Centralized Management ensures efficiency; instead of every owner trying to run the store, they elect a Board of Directors to make the big decisions.

    Classification by Number of Incorporators

    • Corporation Sole: Formed for the purpose of administering and managing, as trustee, the affairs, property, and temporalities of any religious denomination.
    • One Person Corporation (OPC): A corporation with a single stockholder.
    • Ordinary Corporation: Formed by multiple incorporators (typically 2 to 15, or more depending on jurisdiction).

    Who can form a Corporation?

    Under the new law, the requirements for who can start a corporation have become very flexible. The traditional Ordinary Corporation requires a group of people (incorporators) to sign the founding documents. Historically, you needed at least five people, but the modern code has relaxed these numbers.

    The One Person Corporation (OPC) is a revolutionary change in Philippine law. It allows a single individual to enjoy the benefits of limited liability without finding partners. The Corporation Sole remains a special religious category, allowing a Bishop or Priest to hold property in the name of the church rather than in their own personal name.

    Special Corporations

    • Educational Corporations: Organized for educational purposes, governing schools, colleges, and universities.
    • Religious Corporations: Organized by religious groups, societies, or sects.

    Non-Commercial Entities

    Not all corporations are built to sell products. Special Corporations are created for purposes that the State deems vital for the public good. Educational Corporations are strictly regulated by the Department of Education or CHED to ensure academic standards are met.

    Religious Corporations provide a way for faith-based groups to have a legal identity. By incorporating, a church can legally sign a lease for a building or open a bank account to manage donations, ensuring the organization outlasts its current leaders.

    Classification by Nationality

    • Domestic Corporation: Incorporated under the laws of the host country.
    • Foreign Corporation: Formed, organized, or existing under any laws other than those of the host country, and whose laws allow local citizens to do business.

    Domestic vs. Foreign

    A corporation's "citizenship" is determined by where it was born (incorporated). A Domestic Corporation is a child of the Philippine SEC and follows all local rules. A Foreign Corporation is created in a different country but may want to do business here.

    To operate legally in the Philippines, a foreign corporation must obtain a "License to do Business" from the SEC. Without this license, they cannot use our court system to sue someone, although they can still be sued by Filipinos. This ensures that foreign entities are accountable to our local laws while they operate within our borders.

    Other Classifications Based on Purpose / Legal Status

    • Public vs. Private: Public entities are formed to govern a portion of the state, while private entities are formed for private aim or benefit.
    • De Jure vs. De Facto: De Jure corporations have strictly complied with all legal requirements. De Facto corporations have a defect in their incorporation but exist in fact and act as a corporation.

    Legal Perfection and Purpose

    When looking at purpose, Public Corporations are those like cities or municipalities created to govern people. Private Corporations are formed by individuals for their own benefit, whether for profit (Stock) or charity (Non-stock).

    From a technical legal standpoint, we look at De Jure and De Facto status. A De Jure corporation has done everything perfectly according to the SEC. A De Facto corporation has a "defect"‚Äîmaybe a missing signature or a typo in the filings‚Äîbut because they acted in good faith, the law recognizes them as a corporation for most business purposes to protect the people who deal with them.

    The "Corporate Veil" is a legal barrier that protects stockholders from being held liable for the corporation's debts. However, this protection is not absolute. If the court finds that the owners are using the company as a "dummy" or "alter ego" to commit fraud, they will pierce the veil.

    Option B is correct because legal personhood is a privilege granted by the State to promote business stability, not to shield criminal activity. Options A, C, and D are perfectly legal scenarios and do not warrant losing limited liability protection.

    SEC Registration Process

    The formal legal existence of a corporation commences only upon the issuance of the Certificate of Incorporation by the SEC.


    • Name Verification: Reserving a distinct and legally permissible corporate name.
    • Document Preparation: Drafting the Articles of Incorporation and By-laws.
    • Online Filing: Submission of signed documents through the SEC online portal.
    • Payment of Fees: Settling filing and legal research fees based on authorized capital.
    • Issuance of Certificate: The official act granting juridical personality to the corporation.

    The Birth of a Corporation

    A corporation does not exist simply because people agree to do business together; it must be formally birthed by the State through the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC).

    The process begins with securing a unique corporate name to prevent public confusion. The founders then draft the necessary documents, primarily the Articles of Incorporation and By-laws, and submit them via the SEC's online portal. The critical moment occurs when the SEC, satisfied with the documents and the payment of filing fees, issues the Certificate of Incorporation. It is exactly at this moment that the corporation gains its separate juridical personality and limited liability protections.

    Articles of Incorporation (AOI)

    The Articles of Incorporation is the basic contract document defining the corporation's charter, powers, and fundamental structure.


    • Corporate Name & Purpose: Identifies the entity and defines the scope of its authorized business activities.
    • Principal Office: Establishes the official legal address within the Philippines.
    • Corporate Term: Grants perpetual existence by default under the Revised Corporation Code.
    • Founders: Lists the names, nationalities, and residences of the incorporators.
    • Capital Structure: Details the authorized capital stock, number of shares, and initial subscriber commitments.

    The Supreme Charter

    The Articles of Incorporation (AOI) is the most important document in a corporation's existence. It acts as a three-way contract: between the State and the corporation, between the corporation and its shareholders, and among the shareholders themselves.

    The AOI dictates what the corporation is legally allowed to do (its purpose). If a corporation engages in business outside of this stated purpose, those acts are considered ultra vires (beyond its powers). The document also locks in the principal office address, which is crucial because it dictates the legal venue where the corporation can be sued or file lawsuits.

    Incorporators & Initial Management

    Incorporators are the signatories to the AOI who bring the corporation into existence, while the Board of Directors manages its operations.

    • Qualifications: Can be natural persons of legal age, partnerships, associations, or other corporations.
    • Number required: Usually 2 to 15 for an ordinary corporation (or just 1 for a One Person Corporation).
    • Shareholding Requirement: Each incorporator of a stock corporation must own or subscribe to at least one (1) share.
    • Initial Directors: The AOI names the interim board responsible for organizational tasks until the first shareholder election.

    Founders and First Managers

    Incorporators are the original founders. Their names and signatures are permanently etched into the Articles of Incorporation. To be an incorporator of a stock corporation, the law strictly requires that you must own or subscribe to at least one share of the capital stock; you cannot found a company you have no stake in.

    Because a newly birthed corporation needs immediate leadership, the AOI also names the Initial Directors. This interim board acts as the caretakers of the company, handling the immediate organizational tasks‚Äîlike opening a bank account and electing the first corporate officers‚Äîuntil the shareholders can hold their first official meeting to vote for a permanent board.

    Understanding Capitalization Terms

    The financial structure of a stock corporation is categorized into authorized, subscribed, and paid-up capital stock.


    • Authorized Capital Stock: The absolute maximum value and number of shares the corporation can issue based on its AOI.
    • Subscribed Capital Stock: The portion of the authorized capital that investors have legally committed to purchase.
    • Paid-up Capital: The actual portion of the subscribed capital that has been paid to the corporate treasury.
    • General Rule: The Revised Corporation Code eliminated minimum subscribed and paid-up requirements for ordinary corporations upon incorporation, subject to special laws.

    The Financial Foundation

    To analyze a corporation's equity, you must understand three distinct layers of capitalization. Authorized Capital Stock is the legal ceiling. It is the absolute maximum number of shares the company is allowed to print and sell, as defined in its Articles of Incorporation.

    [Image illustrating a funnel: Large Authorized Capital pouring into a smaller Subscribed Capital, pouring into an even smaller Paid-up Capital]

    Subscribed Capital Stock represents the legally binding promises made by investors to buy a portion of those authorized shares. Finally, Paid-up Capital is the hard reality‚Äîit represents the actual cash or property that has already been handed over to the corporate treasury by the subscribers. Under the modernized code, the old rules requiring 25% subscription and 25% paid-up capital upon formation have generally been eliminated to encourage business creation.

    Post-Incorporation Requirements

    SEC registration provides legal existence, but a corporation must comply with other government agency requirements before commencing commercial operations.

    • Adoption of By-laws: Must be filed within the prescribed period to govern internal corporate administration.
    • BIR Registration: Securing a Tax Identification Number (TIN), registering books of accounts, and Authority to Print (ATP) invoices.
    • Local Government Units (LGU): Obtaining Barangay Clearance and the Mayor's / Business Permit.
    • Statutory Benefits: Registering as an employer with SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG.
    • Secondary Licenses: Procuring specialized permits from agencies (e.g., BSP, Insurance Commission) if entering regulated industries.

    Beyond the SEC Certificate

    Obtaining the SEC Certificate of Incorporation grants the corporation legal life, but it does not grant the right to immediately open shop and sell goods. A series of secondary compliances must be met.

    Internally, the corporation must adopt its By-laws, which are the internal rules governing how meetings are run and officers are elected. Externally, the corporation must register with the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) to legally issue invoices, and with the Local Government Unit (LGU) for a Mayor's Permit. Finally, to protect future employees, it must register with statutory benefit agencies like SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG.

    Day 2: Corporate Equity and Accounting Methods


    • To remember the fundamental rights of a shareholder and the different classes of corporate stock (Common, Preferred, Founders').
    • To understand the legal mechanisms and restrictions involved in managing capital through Treasury and Redeemable shares.
    • To analyze the differences between the Memorandum Method and the Journal Entry Method when recording equity transactions.

    Learning Objectives

    In Day 2, we shift our focus to the financial engine of the corporation: its equity structure. You cannot analyze a corporation without understanding the units of ownership that fund it.

    Students will be expected to remember the bundle of legal rights attached to shares and the specific privileges (or limitations) of different stock classes. We will ensure you understand how a corporation legally buys back its own stock. Most importantly for accountants, we will deeply analyze the journal entries used to track these transactions, comparing the Memorandum Method against the formal Journal Entry Method.

    Shares of Stock & Basic Shareholder Rights


    • Nature: Represents a unit of ownership and the stockholder's interest in the corporation.
    • Voting Rights: The right to participate in management by electing directors and approving major corporate acts.
    • Dividend Rights: The right to share in surplus profits when declared by the Board.
    • Pre-emptive Rights: The right to subscribe to new share issuances to prevent dilution of ownership.
    • Inspection & Liquidation: The right to inspect books and share in remaining assets after dissolution.

    The Bundle of Rights

    A share of stock is more than just a piece of paper; it represents a fractional ownership interest in the corporation. Owning a share grants the investor a specific "bundle of rights."

    These rights include the power to vote for the Board of Directors, ensuring the owners maintain ultimate control. Shareholders also have the right to receive a portion of the profits (dividends) when declared. To protect their percentage of ownership from shrinking when new shares are issued, they hold Pre-emptive Rights, allowing them first refusal to buy the new stock. Furthermore, they maintain the right to inspect corporate records to prevent management fraud, and the right to a share of whatever is left over if the corporation is dissolved and liquidated.

    Common Shares

    Common shares represent the basic, residual ownership of the corporation without any fixed guarantees on returns.


    • Residual Ownership: Holders bear the ultimate risk of loss but enjoy the maximum potential for growth.
    • Dividend Priority: Dividends are paid only after all preferred share obligations are satisfied.
    • Voting Power: Inherently possesses complete and unrestricted voting rights.
    • Liquidation Claim: Last in line to receive distributions if the corporation is dissolved and liquidated.

    The Backbone of Equity

    Common Shares are the standard unit of corporate ownership. The law refers to them as holding "residual ownership." This means that in both dividend payouts and company liquidation, common shareholders stand at the very back of the line. All creditors, bondholders, and preferred shareholders must be paid before a common shareholder sees a single peso.

    Why buy them? Because their upside is unlimited. While preferred shares might be capped at a 5% return, if the company invents the next smartphone, the common shareholders absorb all the massive excess profits. Furthermore, because they bear the most risk, corporate law dictates that common shares must always possess full voting rights to control the company's destiny.

    Preferred Shares

    Preferred shares grant specific advantages in dividends and liquidation but generally restrict standard management control.

    • Dividend Preference: Entitled to receive dividends before common shareholders.
    • Liquidation Preference: Priority claim over common shares on remaining assets during dissolution.
    • Voting Restrictions: Generally non-voting for the Board of Directors, but retain voting rights on fundamental corporate changes.
    • Par Value Requirement: The law mandates that preferred shares must always be issued with a stated par value.

    Trading Control for Security

    Corporations issue Preferred Shares to attract conservative investors who want safer returns. A preferred share acts as a hybrid between stock and a bond. The defining feature is the "preference"‚Äîmeaning these shareholders get their declared dividends before the common stockholders get anything.

    However, this financial security comes at a cost to corporate control. Preferred shares are almost always issued as non-voting shares. While they cannot vote for the daily management (the Board of Directors), the law protects them by allowing them to vote on massive, fundamental changes, like selling all corporate assets or amending the corporate charter. Legally, preferred shares must always be issued with a stated par value.

    Redeemable & Treasury Shares

    • Redeemable Shares: Shares the corporation can buy back at a fixed date or event, regardless of unrestricted retained earnings (provided it doesn't cause insolvency).
    • Treasury Shares: Shares previously issued and fully paid, then reacquired by the corporation.
    • Rights Suspended: Treasury shares have no voting rights and are not entitled to dividends while held by the corporation.
    • Acquisition Limits: Acquiring treasury shares strictly requires the corporation to have sufficient unrestricted retained earnings.

    Managing the Capital Structure

    Sometimes a corporation needs to buy back its own shares. Treasury Shares are ordinary shares that were fully paid for, but the corporation bought them back. While sitting in the corporate "treasury," these shares are dormant‚Äîthey earn no dividends and cannot be voted. However, to protect the company's creditors, a corporation can only buy treasury shares if it has enough profits (Unrestricted Retained Earnings) to afford them.

    Redeemable Shares are different. They are issued with a pre-planned expiration date where the company has the right (or obligation) to buy them back. Because investors know this from day one, the law allows the corporation to redeem them even if there are no retained earnings, provided the buyback doesn't bankrupt the company.

    Founders' Shares

    Shares issued to organizers and promoters to reward them for establishing the corporate enterprise.

    • Exclusive Privileges: Granted special rights not given to other classes of stock.
    • Board Control: Commonly given the exclusive right to vote and be voted as members of the Board of Directors.
    • Time Limitation: The exclusive right to elect directors is strictly limited to a maximum of five (5) years from the date of incorporation.
    • Reversion: After the five-year period, founders' shares generally share the same voting rights as ordinary common shares.

    Rewarding the Visionaries

    Building a corporation from nothing is difficult and risky. To reward the original organizers, the law allows the creation of Founders' Shares. These shares can be granted exclusive privileges that normal common stock does not possess.

    The most powerful privilege granted is usually the exclusive right to elect the Board of Directors. This ensures the founders retain complete control over the company's direction during its critical startup phase, even if they sell off majority ownership to outside investors. However, to prevent permanent dictatorships, the SEC strictly limits this exclusive voting right to a maximum of five (5) years. After that, the shares revert to normal voting power.

    • D) Their voting rights are doubled to give the Board of Directors more control.
    Correct Answer: B. Their voting rights and dividend entitlements are suspended while held by the corporation.

    Review: Dormant Shares

    The correct answer is B. It would be illogical for a corporation to pay dividends to itself or to allow the management board to vote using the company's own shares to keep themselves in power. Therefore, while in the treasury, the shares exist but are entirely dormant.

    Checkpoint Question 15: Founders' Shares

    Founders' shares are often granted the exclusive privilege to vote and be voted as members of the Board of Directors. Under the Revised Corporation Code, what is the strict limitation placed on this exclusive right?

    • A) It expires exactly 10 years from the date of the first board meeting.
    • B) It cannot exceed a period of five (5) years from the date of incorporation.
    • C) It is perpetually valid as long as the founder holds at least one share.
    • D) It expires immediately once the corporation declares its first cash dividend.
    Correct Answer: B. It cannot exceed a period of five (5) years from the date of incorporation.

    Review: Limiting Founder Control

    The correct answer is B. The law balances the need to reward founders with the need for corporate democracy. Giving founders an absolute dictatorship forever would harm public investors. Therefore, the exclusive right to elect the board is strictly capped at five years.

    Recording Equity: Two Methods

    Corporations use two primary methods to record authorized, subscribed, and issued capital stock.


    • The Memorandum Method: The most widely used approach in practice; relies on descriptive memos for authorization.
    • The Journal Entry Method: Uses formal ledger accounts to track the total authorized and unissued shares.
    • Shared Outcome: Both methods ultimately yield the exact same Total Stockholders' Equity presentation.
    • Clarification: "Paid-Up Capital" is not a formal account title, but rather the sum of Share Capital and Additional Paid-in Capital (APIC).

    The Mechanics of Equity Accounting

    When a corporation raises funds by selling shares, the accounting department must meticulously track three stages: Authorization, Subscription, and Issuance. In the Philippines, this is done using either the Memorandum Method or the Journal Entry Method.

    While the mechanical debits and credits differ slightly between the two, it is vital to remember that both methods arrive at the exact same Total Stockholders' Equity on the Balance Sheet. A common point of confusion for students is the term "Paid-up Capital." You will never write a journal entry that debits cash and credits "Paid-up Capital." Instead, that term is an umbrella concept representing the combined balances of the Share Capital account and the Additional Paid-in Capital account.

    Proforma: Authorization of Capital Stock


    Memorandum Method Journal Entry Method
    (No formal journal entry)

    Memo: The corporation was authorized to issue [X] ordinary shares with a par value of ‚Ç±[Y].     		Dr. Unissued Capital Stock
        Cr. Authorized Capital Stock

    To record the authorized capitalization of the corporation.

    Recording the Authorization

    When the SEC issues the Certificate of Incorporation, the company is officially granted the power to sell shares. The two methods treat this initial event very differently.

    The Memorandum Method is popular because it is simple: no financial transaction has occurred yet, so no debits or credits are made. The accountant merely writes a sticky note (a memo) in the ledger stating the legal facts. The Journal Entry Method, however, takes a formal approach. It immediately establishes the ceiling of the company's equity by crediting Authorized Capital Stock and simultaneously debiting Unissued Capital Stock, essentially creating a tracking pool of shares waiting to be sold.

    Proforma: Subscription of Capital Stock


    Memorandum Method Journal Entry Method
    Dr. Subscriptions Receivable
        Cr. Subscribed Capital Stock
        Cr. Additional Paid-in Capital (if above par)     		Dr. Subscriptions Receivable
        Cr. Subscribed Capital Stock
        Cr. Additional Paid-in Capital (if above par)

    Note: The entry to record subscriptions is identical under both methods.

    Recording the Subscription

    A subscription is a legally binding promise by an investor to buy stock. Because the promise is legally enforceable, it creates an asset for the company: Subscriptions Receivable.

    This entry is the one time the Memorandum and Journal Entry methods are exactly identical. A critical rule to master here is the handling of premiums. If a share has a par value of ‚Ç±100, but the investor agrees to buy it for ‚Ç±150, the extra ‚Ç±50 is a premium. The accountant must credit the Additional Paid-in Capital account for that ‚Ç±50 immediately upon subscription, not later when the cash is collected. The economic benefit is locked in the moment the contract is signed.

    Payment & Issuance (At Par / Without APIC)


    Memorandum Method Journal Entry Method
    To record full payment:
    Dr. Cash
        Cr. Subscriptions Receivable     		To record full payment:
    Dr. Cash
        Cr. Subscriptions Receivable
    To record issuance of stock certificates:
    Dr. Subscribed Capital Stock
        Cr. Capital Stock (or Share Capital)     		To record issuance of stock certificates:
    Dr. Subscribed Capital Stock
        Cr. Unissued Capital Stock

    Clearing the Receivable and Issuing Stock

    When the subscriber finally pays cash, the entry is simple: Debit Cash, Credit Subscriptions Receivable. This is true under both methods.

    However, once the balance reaches zero, the company must issue the physical stock certificates. This requires clearing out the temporary Subscribed Capital Stock account. Under the Memorandum Method, the stock is finalized by crediting the main Capital Stock account directly. Under the Journal Entry Method, the accountant credits Unissued Capital Stock, thereby shrinking the pool of available shares established in step one.

    Payment & Issuance (Above Par / With APIC)

    If shares are subscribed at a premium, the APIC is already recorded during subscription. The payment and issuance entries remain focused on clearing the receivable and converting the stock.


    Memorandum Method Journal Entry Method
    To record full payment (including premium):
    Dr. Cash
        Cr. Subscriptions Receivable     		To record full payment (including premium):
    Dr. Cash
        Cr. Subscriptions Receivable
    To record issuance of stock certificates:
    Dr. Subscribed Capital Stock
        Cr. Capital Stock (at par value)     		To record issuance of stock certificates:
    Dr. Subscribed Capital Stock
        Cr. Unissued Capital Stock (at par value)

    Issuance Involving Premiums

    If you understood the subscription phase, this step is easy. Because the Additional Paid-in Capital (Premium) was securely recorded when the contract was signed, the subsequent payment entries ignore it entirely.

    When the subscriber hands over the cash, the accountant debits Cash for the full amount (Par + Premium) and credits the Receivable. When the certificate is issued, the accountant debits Subscribed Capital Stock and credits either Capital Stock (Memo) or Unissued Capital Stock (Journal). This issuance entry is made strictly at the par value of the stock, keeping the par value separate from the premium in the ledgers.

    Key Takeaways


    • Timing of APIC: Additional Paid-in Capital is formally recognized at the time of subscription, not issuance.
    • Practical Preference: The Memorandum Method is the most widely used approach in the real-world Philippine accounting setting due to its simplicity.
    • Financial Presentation: Both methods result in the exact same Stockholders' Equity figures on the Balance Sheet.
    • Calculating Issued Capital:
      • Memo Method: Issued Capital = Ending balance of the Capital Stock account.
      • Journal Method: Issued Capital = Authorized Capital Stock minus Unissued Capital Stock.

    Summary of Equity Methods

    While the mechanical steps of the Memorandum and Journal Entry methods differ, the destination is the same. Real-world accountants vastly prefer the Memorandum Method because it avoids creating unnecessary "dummy" accounts like Unissued Capital Stock.

    However, understanding both is essential for CPA board examinations. If you are analyzing a balance sheet built with the Journal Entry Method, you must know how to calculate the true issued capital: you take the static Authorized Capital Stock limit and subtract whatever is left sitting in the Unissued Capital Stock pool.
