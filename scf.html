1. Overview and Purpose

Definition

The statement of cash flows summarizes the cash and cash equivalents that enter and leave a company. It removes non-cash transactions to show the actual cash available during a specific period.

Purpose & Role

The statement of cash flows has a clear purpose in financial reporting by showing actual cash movements instead of complex accruals.[1] Regulators require this report to remove accounting estimates and give standard measurements.[1] It stops companies from hiding cash losses behind aggressive revenue reporting.[2] By dividing activities into operating, investing, and financing, it becomes very useful for business stakeholders.[3] In the end, this statement acts as a reliable guide to check if a company can survive and pay its debts.[2]

  • Assesses the entity's ability to generate cash to pay obligations.

    Checking if a company can generate cash is the most important step for continuous business operations.[4] Accrual accounting can sometimes hide cash problems by showing revenues that are not yet collected.[5] By looking only at actual cash inflows, analysts can see if a business can truly support itself.[2] Banks heavily rely on this cash flow measure to check if a company is a good credit risk.[4] A growing cash flow gives solid proof that the business is financially strong and stable.[4]

  • Reconciles accrual-based Net Income to actual cash flow.

    This matching process bridges the gap caused by non-cash accounting methods.[5] The reported net income naturally includes big non-cash deductions like depreciation.[5] The reconciliation process removes these accounting adjustments to show the real changes in working capital.[5] Auditors rely on this comparison to easily spot if a company is manipulating its earnings.[5] It proves that while the income statement shows profit, only actual cash can be used to pay dividends.[5]

  • Evaluates the financial structure (debt vs. equity).

    Checking the balance between debt and equity gives deep insights into how a company funds its operations.[2] By tracking cash from loans versus issuing shares, stakeholders can see the company's changing risk levels.[2] Relying too much on borrowed money brings big risks because of the strict interest payments required.[2] On the other hand, getting cash from stock shares shows a plan to save cash by avoiding strict loan rules.[2] This structural check is a very basic foundation of modern corporate finance analysis.[2]

  • Predicts future cash flows based on historical performance.

    Using past audited data to guess future cash flows is a very important tool for planning.[2] Even though the statement looks at the past, its cash trends are used as a base for valuing the company.[2] Financial analysts depend on these past records to guess when and how sure future cash will come in.[2] Knowing the normal operating cash helps managers safely plan for future big projects.[2] This ability to predict makes the cash flow statement a very needed tool for strategic business planning.[2]

2. Cash and Cash Equivalents

In actual financial reporting, companies do not list every single bank account or petty cash box directly on the face of the balance sheet. Instead, they group all these highly liquid assets into one single line item simply called "Cash and Cash Equivalents". This grouping makes the main financial report look clean and very easy to read for the users. If investors want to know the exact breakdown of this total amount, they must look at the attached Notes to Financial Statements. This attached note will clearly show how much is physical cash, how much is resting in the bank, and how much is placed in short-term investments.

The PAS 7 Criteria

PAS 7 sets clear rules for defining true cash equivalents using a strict standard.[6] This rule is made to stop company treasurers from faking their available cash.[6] By asking for strict compliance, it makes sure only the safest assets are grouped with real money.[6] Regulators strictly enforce these limits to keep the company's balance sheet honest and accurate.[6] These rules act as a strict test to separate real cash equivalents from risky investments.[6]

  1. Short-term and highly liquid.

    The rule that an instrument must be short-term and highly liquid sets a strict time limit.[6] PAS 7 clearly defines 'short-term' as having a maturity of exactly three months or less.[6] This time limit is strictly counted from the exact date it was bought, not the original issue date.[6] The 'highly liquid' tag means the asset can be quickly sold in an active market.[6] These two rules make sure the funds are always ready to pay off sudden business debts.[6]

  2. Readily convertible to known amounts of cash.

    The strict rule for easy conversion aims to remove all doubts about the asset's value.[6] To meet this rule, the company must know the exact amount of cash they will get when they sell it.[6] Any financial asset with a complicated or changing price totally fails this strict test.[6] This required certainty acts as a strong block against volatile trading assets that offer unsure cash amounts.[6] It guarantees that the total balance sheet number shows highly reliable buying power.[6]

  3. Subject to an insignificant risk of changes in value.

    The last rule serves as a strong safety measure to protect cash funds from sudden market crashes.[6] An investment that is easy to sell but easily loses its value is simply rejected from being a cash equivalent.[6] The phrase 'insignificant risk' practically limits this group to very safe investments like government treasury bills.[6] Traded stock prices are naturally tied to changing market moods and cannot meet this strict rule.[6] Demanding almost total capital protection ensures the company's emergency funds are safe from global financial problems.[6]

Core Rule: To qualify, an investment must have a maturity of three months or less from its acquisition date.

Examples and Common Constraints

Listing practical examples of corporate assets serves as a great guide for applying PAS 7 rules.[6] By comparing good instruments with bad ones, this list creates a very helpful and easy comparison.[6] This selection targets the most common reporting mistakes by showing complex products that look like real cash.[6] Trained accountants use this detailed list to quickly check large company portfolios for wrongly classified investments.[6] These specific examples make it easier to understand the strict mix of maturity, liquidity, and risk limits.[6]

  • Petty Cash Fund: ADJUSTED INCLUSION - Depends on the contents.

    A Petty Cash Fund (PCF) is a small amount of cash kept on hand for minor expenses like office supplies or taxi fares. While the fund itself is part of 'Cash,' you must "clean" the balance at year-end. Only the actual currencies and coins remaining in the box are included in Cash and Cash Equivalents.

    Items to EXCLUDE:
    • Unreplenished Vouchers: These represent expenses already paid (e.g., snacks, postage). They must be reported as Expenses, not cash.
    • Employee IOUs (Vale): These are personal advances to employees and must be reported as Receivables.
    • Post-dated Checks: If an employee puts a future-dated check in the box in exchange for cash, it is not yet "money." This is reported as a Receivable.

    Pro-tip: If the fund is not replenished at year-end, the total "Cash" on the balance sheet will be overstated unless you make an adjusting entry to remove these non-cash items.

  • Savings Account: INCLUDED - Highly liquid and readily available.

    A Savings Account is an interest-bearing deposit account held at a bank. While it is primarily intended for accumulating funds rather than daily spending, it is considered Cash because the funds can be withdrawn at any time without prior notice or significant penalty.

    Key Characteristic: Even if the bank technically has the right to require a notice period for withdrawals (a common but rarely enforced banking rule), the account remains part of Cash and Cash Equivalents due to its immediate accessibility for meeting short-term obligations.

  • Current Account (Checking or Demand Deposit): INCLUDED - The primary source of operating cash.

    Commonly referred to as a Checking Account or Demand Deposit, this is the primary account used by businesses for daily operations. It is called a "Demand Deposit" because the depositor has the legal right to "demand" the funds immediately by writing a check, using a debit card, or making an electronic transfer.

    Accounting Treatment: These accounts are the most liquid form of bank deposits and are always reported under Cash.

    Note on Overdrafts: If a Current Account falls below zero, the negative balance is reported as a Bank Overdraft (a Current Liability) and is generally not netted against positive balances in other banks.

  • 30-Day Treasury Bill: INCLUDED - Short-term, highly liquid.

    The 30-day Philippine Treasury Bill is widely known by auditors as the perfect example of a cash equivalent.[6] Issued directly by the national government, it has almost zero chance of default, perfectly meeting the risk rule.[6] Its short 30-day life easily falls within the strict three-month maximum limit counted from the date of purchase.[6] The very active local market for these securities makes sure they can be instantly changed to exact cash amounts.[6] Company treasurers heavily use these government instruments as the solid base for their ready cash funds.[6]

  • Investment in Equity: EXCLUDED - High risk of value change.

    Investments directly in changing stock shares are strictly banned from the cash equivalents category.[6] They basically break the main rule of keeping the capital safe, which is required by the reporting standard.[6] Buying shares gives an ownership stake, making the asset's price very sensitive to changing market cycles and moods.[6] This natural changing value means complex stock investments are always at a high risk of losing their worth.[6] Because of this, all normal stock investments must be clearly separated as trading or available-for-sale assets.[6]

  • 1-Year Time Deposit: EXCLUDED - Maturity > 3 months.

    The standard one-year bank time deposit shows how strict the time limits of PAS 7 really are.[6] Even though it is considered very safe with almost no risk of losing value, this bank product fails the maturity test.[6] Because its original contract length is much longer than the three-month limit from purchase, it cannot be a cash equivalent.[6] Even if the reporting date happens just one month before it matures, the classification stays fixed from the purchase date.[6] These specific investments must be accurately separated and reported as short-term investments on the balance sheet.[6]

  • Bank Overdrafts: LIABILITY - Not offset against cash.

    Bank overdrafts happen when you withdraw more money than what is inside your bank account. In the Philippines, we do not normally minus this from our other cash balances. Instead, we report this overdraft as a current liability because it is basically a short-term loan from the bank. You can only offset it if you have another account in the exact same bank and they strictly allow it. Always keep it separate to clearly show the true debt of the company.

  • Restricted Cash: EXCLUDED - Not available for daily use.

    Restricted cash refers to funds set aside for a specific purpose that cannot be used for immediate or general operations. Common examples include legally restricted compensating balances (mandated by a bank for a loan), sinking funds (cash saved specifically to pay off long-term bonds), and escrow accounts (funds held for a specific legal transaction or tax payment).

    Classification Rule: If the restriction is for more than one year (e.g., for a future plant expansion), it is reported as a Non-current Asset. If the restriction is short-term, it is reported as a Current Asset, but it is never combined with 'Cash and Cash Equivalents.' This ensures the balance sheet provides an honest look at the company’s actual liquidity.

  • Postdated Checks: RECEIVABLE - Cannot be encashed yet.

    Postdated checks are checks given by a customer but with a date set in the future. Even if you physically hold the check today, you cannot encash it yet at the bank. Because you still cannot use the money, you must not include it in your cash balance. You should just keep it as part of your accounts receivable until the actual date arrives. This makes sure we only count the real money we can spend right now.

  • Employee IOUs / Advances: RECEIVABLE - Not real purchasing power.

    Employee IOUs are papers signed by workers showing they borrowed money from the company. Sometimes the cashier keeps these papers inside the cash box and wrongly counts them as money. However, these are just receivables and not real cash that you can use to buy things. You must remove them from the cash count and record them as advances to employees instead. It is very wrong to pretend these papers have the same buying power as actual peso bills.

  • Foreign Currency Accounts: INCLUDED - Translated at closing rate.

    Many local companies keep dollar or other foreign money in special bank accounts. When making the financial statements, you must always translate this foreign money into Philippine Pesos. You should use the exchange rate exactly on the date of the balance sheet, not the date you deposited it. If the exchange rate goes up or down, you will have an unrealized gain or loss. Even with changing rates, this is still part of cash as long as you can withdraw it anytime.

3. Classification of Cash Flows

Operating Activities

Principal revenue-producing activities.

Operating activities show the main economic engine of a company by tracking cash from its main business tasks.[7] Separating these daily transactions from investments gives users a clear view of the business's true health.[8] The clear split of operating cash inflows and outflows explains how the company supports its daily needs.[8] Big investors closely check this category because steady operating cash is the best sign of long-term survival.[8] Through this breakdown, the broad idea of cash flow is turned into clear, measurable transaction groups.[8]

  • (+) Inflows: Sale of goods, royalties, fees.

    Operating cash inflows represent the real financial lifeblood of any commercial business.[8] They include the physical cash collected directly from main revenue activities, like the daily sale of goods.[8] Other good income streams, like royalties and software fees, also come directly from the company's main skills.[8] These specific daily inflows are very different from one-time asset sales because they show continuous market demand.[8] A steadily growing stream of operating inflows proves the business can fund its own growth.[8]

  • (-) Outflows: Payments to suppliers, employees.

    Operating cash outflows list the necessary expenses needed to keep the main business running smoothly.[8] Actual cash payments sent to suppliers for raw materials show the direct costs tied to making sales.[8] At the same time, regular cash payments given to the workers represent the human effort needed for daily operations.[8] This outflow group also includes normal daily expenses like office utility bills and short-term equipment rentals.[8] Carefully managing how fast these outflows happen compared to cash collections is the main goal for keeping good liquidity.[8]

Investing Activities

Acquisition and disposal of long-term assets.

Investing activities clearly show the long-term cash effects of a company's big decisions on spending its capital.[7] By grouping the buying and selling of long-term assets, this list separates future growth from daily tasks.[8] This breakdown is very important to see exactly how managers use extra cash to grow the business or earn returns.[8] Tracking these cash movements helps analysts see if a company is physically growing or just staying the same size.[8] In the end, this reporting section tells a clear story about the company's long-term goals and spending habits.[8]

  • (+) Inflows: Sale of PPE, debt/equity instruments.

    Investing cash inflows mostly come from strategically selling valuable long-term assets and other company investments.[6] Selling extra property, plant, and equipment brings in a big amount of cash that is totally separate from normal sales.[6] Selling bonds or stock shares held in other companies shows the closing of past investment deals.[6] These rare cash inflows become very important to watch during times when a company is heavily reorganizing.[6] Relying too much on selling assets just to pay for basic daily salaries usually points to a huge underlying business problem.[6]

  • (-) Outflows: Purchase of PPE, loans made.

    Investing cash outflows represent very heavy long-term financial promises made to gain profitable future benefits.[6] The huge cash purchase of modern property, plant, and equipment are key spending needed to replace old machines.[6] This category clearly includes cash spent to buy the bonds or shares of other outside companies.[6] Giving out physical cash advances and long-term business loans to other parties also strictly counts as an investing outflow.[6] These huge cash drains are usually seen as a good sign by investors as long as they promise better future returns.[6]

Financing Activities

Changes in size/composition of equity and borrowings.

The list for financing activities clearly explains how a company changes its basic capital structure.[7] It groups transactions that directly change the total amount and mix of the company's equity and debts.[6] This clear outline is very helpful for users who need to strictly check company debt levels and dividend payouts.[2] By showing exactly where outside funds come from and how they are paid back, it gives a clear view of financial planning.[2] As a result, it acts as a very important tool for checking long-term risks and if the funding strategy is sustainable.[2]

  • (+) Inflows: Proceeds from issuing shares, loans.

    Financing cash inflows accurately record the entry of fresh new capital into the business straight from outside investors.[6] Huge cash amounts gained from issuing new common or preferred shares represent pure equity financing.[6] Equity financing naturally lessens the current owners' control but safely gets vital funds without strict future repayment dates.[6] On the other hand, getting physical cash from issuing bonds or taking bank loans is considered pure debt financing.[6] While debt gives quick needed cash, it also creates forced future payments that permanently change the company's long-term risk.[6]

  • (-) Outflows: Loan repayments, treasury stock, dividends.

    Financing cash outflows accurately show the physical cash taken from the treasury just to pay outside investors and lenders.[6] The required payment of borrowed loan amounts perfectly shows a serious effort to reduce company debt.[6] Cash used to buy back the company's own shares indicates a planned program to increase ownership control.[6] The regular payment of cash dividends represents a real sharing of extra profits strictly with the stockholders.[6] A steady ability to make these huge payments over many years without hurting daily operations is the sign of a strong company.[6]

4. Special Treatments (PAS 7)

Bank Overdrafts

Generally financing activities. In the PH, they are not permitted to be cash equivalents and are strictly current liability/financing.

Interest and Dividends

The accounting treatment for interest and dividends shows a widely debated and flexible part of PAS 7.[1] Unlike standard cash flow groups with strict rules, PAS 7 allows companies to use professional judgment to group these cash flows.[1] This flexibility respects that the real economic meaning of these items changes a lot depending on the type of business.[1] The standard gives two possible choices for each transaction, serving as a helpful guide for reading financial reports.[1] Because of this, smart users must carefully read the required accounting notes to understand the exact logic used by the company.[1]

  • Interest Paid: Operating/Financing

    The official grouping of interest payments mostly depends on how the company's management views the cost of borrowing.[1] Usually, most normal trading companies properly record interest paid simply as an operating cash outflow.[1] This is because it directly affects the exact calculation of net profit on the income statement.[1] However, an equally correct choice under PAS 7 is to report this big expense clearly as a financing activity.[1] On the hand, banks and lending companies always record interest paid as operating cash flows since managing interest is their main business.[1]

  • Interest Received: Operating/Investing

    The physical cash coming from collected interest gives another chance for a dual classification under the standard.[1] Grouping this cash strictly as an operating activity is very common because the earnings directly add to net income.[1] As another option, smart managers might carefully choose to report interest received clearly as an investing cash inflow.[1] This specific view perfectly matches the physical cash received directly with the long-term asset that produced it.[1] For central banks and specialized lenders, interest received is strictly seen as a pure operating cash inflow.[1]

  • Dividends Paid: Financing/Operating

    The big payout of cash dividends to shareholders needs very careful thought about where to put it in the statement.[1] The most common global practice is to classify dividends paid strictly as a financing cash outflow.[1] This strict grouping clearly separates the sharing of profits from the daily normal expenses needed to earn them.[1] Even so, PAS 7 still allows certain specific companies to record these big cash dividends paid as an operating cash flow.[1] This other choice helps users check the company's basic physical ability to pay dividends strictly from its daily operations.[1]

  • Dividends Received: Operating/Investing

    Physical cash received straight from profitable dividend payouts gives another important point of choice within the rules.[1] Actively putting these received dividends into standard operating activities is usually because they are included in net income.[1] On the other hand, purposely reporting them as investing cash inflows argues that they are returns on money invested outside.[1] This investing view perfectly matches the cash return with the huge original cash spent to buy the shares.[1] Just like all flexible choices in PAS 7, the chosen method must truly and steadily show the company's real economic situation.[1]

Non-Cash Transactions

Excluded from the body of the CF statement. Disclosed in notes (e.g., converting bonds to shares).

5. Reporting Methods: Indirect

Adjusts accrual Net Income for non-cash effects, deferrals, and accruals.

Step-by-Step Procedure

The clear list detailing the indirect method gives a step-by-step mathematical way to connect accrual accounting with actual cash.[5] By strictly using a set structure, it dictates the exact order that accountants must perfectly follow.[5] This specific method is widely preferred because it easily uses audited data straight from the income statement and balance sheet.[5] As a result, it completely removes the hard work of tracing every single small cash transaction made during the year.[5] The structured list acts as an exact diagnostic tool, successfully separating non-cash items from reported earnings to show real cash.[5]

  1. Start with Net Income: From the Income Statement.

    The indirect reconciliation process strictly needs a clear starting point, which is widely known as the net income figure.[5] This heavily checked number shows the overall business performance under accrual accounting, regardless of real cash movements.[5] By using this theoretical number as the base, the indirect method clearly respects the importance of the income statement.[5] The reported net income naturally includes both pure cash sales and big non-cash accounting adjustments.[5] Setting this exact starting point is the basic need before making the many adjustments to find the true operating cash flow.[5]

  2. Add back Non-Cash Exp: e.g., Depreciation.

    The first important mathematical adjustment involves adding back huge accounting expenses that lowered the reported net income.[5] Big depreciation and asset amortization are the most common examples, representing the regular spread of past asset costs.[5] Because these non-cash expenses fake a lower net income, they must be mathematically added back in full.[5] This specific adjustment also covers other non-cash charges like natural resource depletion, big asset value losses, and bad debt allowances.[5] By doing this careful step, analysts effectively remove the huge accounting effects tied to using long-term assets.[5]

  3. Adjust Non-Operating Gains/Losses: Deduct gains, add losses from asset sales to avoid double counting.

    This middle step carefully removes items that affect net income but totally do not belong in operating activities.[5] When a company sells a long-term asset for a big profit, the resulting gain is included in net income.[5] To avoid the huge mistake of counting this cash twice, these accounting gains must be strictly deducted from net income.[5] On the other hand, accounting losses on asset sales must be added back simply because they lowered net income without using cash.[5] This careful removal ensures that the final operating cash figure stays completely free from investing or financing transactions.[5]

  4. Adjust Working Capital:

    The final and most active phase involves checking the big yearly changes in short-term operating assets and current liabilities.[5] Huge working capital adjustments are mathematically needed because accrual rules record sales long before real cash is collected.[5] This specific step perfectly fixes the big timing differences between recording revenue and actually getting the physical cash.[5] By tracking changes in the balance sheet, the process accurately counts the huge amount of cash stuck in unsold goods.[5] Mastering this step is needed, as bad working capital management is often the hidden cause for sudden company cash crises.[5]

    Working Capital Adjustment Rules:

    The specific sub-list for working capital gives the exact math rules needed to perfectly finish the indirect method.[5] It works exactly as a strict two-way guide, telling precisely how changes in current assets and liabilities affect the final number.[5] This smaller list is definitely needed because it directly turns accounting balance sheet changes into real cash flow effects.[5] By strictly separating operating assets from operating liabilities, it makes the complex movement of short-term cash easier to handle.[5] These specific rules make sure that analysts correctly read whether working capital changes brought in cash or used up cash.[5]

    • Current Assets: Inc = Deduct, Dec = Add.

      This specific fixed rule strictly controls the complex cash movements of short-term operating assets like receivables and inventory.[5] When huge current assets increase over a year, it clearly means that the company has used up actual physical cash.[5] Therefore, this massive asset increase is a big use of cash and must be mathematically deducted from the net income.[5] Conversely, a big decrease shows that inventory was sold or receivables were collected, turning unused assets back into cash.[5] Because of this, such huge asset decreases act as a big source of physical cash and must be added to net income.[5]

    • Current Liab: Inc = Add, Dec = Deduct.

      This final rule perfectly explains the deep cash effects of actively managing short-term debts like payables and unpaid wages.[5] A huge increase in current liabilities strongly shows that the company has saved cash by delaying payments to its suppliers.[5] Because this delayed payment works like a free short-term loan from suppliers, it heavily increases immediate company cash.[5] In clear contrast, a sudden drop in current liabilities means the company used its cash to quickly pay short-term debts.[5] As a strict mathematical result, this drop in debt is a sure physical cash outflow and must be deducted from net income.[5]

Step-by-Step Procedure

  1. Cash from Customers:
    Cash Collected = Sales + Decrease (or - Inc) in AR

    To find out how much actual money we collected from our buyers, we start with the sales revenue from the income statement. We then look at the accounts receivable to see if it increased or decreased during the year. If the accounts receivable went down, it means customers paid their old debts, so we add that decrease to our sales. If the accounts receivable went up, it means customers did not pay some of their purchases, so we subtract that increase. This simple formula makes sure we only count the real cash that entered our bank account.

    Example Computation: Cash from Customers

    Reported Sales Revenue: 500,000
    Less: Increase in Accounts Receivable: (10,000)
    Cash Collected from Customers: 490,000
  2. Cash to Suppliers:
    Cash Paid = COGS + Increase (or - Dec) in Inventory - Increase (or + Dec) in AP

    Calculating the cash given to our suppliers needs two important adjustments starting from the cost of goods sold. First, we check our inventory to see if we bought more goods than we actually sold. If the inventory increased, we add the increase because we used cash to buy those extra stocks. Second, we look at our accounts payable to see if we delayed payments to our suppliers. If the accounts payable increased, we subtract the increase because we kept our cash instead of paying them right away.

    Example Computation: Cash to Suppliers

    Cost of Goods Sold (COGS): 300,000
    Less: Decrease in Inventory: (10,000)
    Add: Decrease in Accounts Payable: 5,000
    Cash Paid to Suppliers: 295,000
  3. Cash for Operating Expenses (General):
    Cash Paid = OpEx (net of non-cash) + Increase (or - Dec) in Prepayments - Increase (or + Dec) in Accruals

    Operating expenses on the income statement often include items that do not use real physical cash. Because of this, we must first remove non-cash expenses like depreciation or bad debts from the total amount. After removing them, we adjust the remaining balance by looking at prepaid and accrued expenses. For example, if our prepaid expenses decreased, it means we used up old payments, so we subtract that decrease. This careful step ensures we are only reporting the exact peso amount we spent to run the business daily.

    Example Computation: Cash for General OpEx

    General Operating Expenses: 80,000
    Less: Increase in Accrued General OpEx: (5,000)
    Cash Paid for General OpEx: 75,000
  4. Payments for Specific Expenses (Prepaid & Accrued):
    Cash Paid = Expense + Increase (or - Dec) Prepaid - Increase (or + Dec) Accrued

    Sometimes, a single expense account like rent is affected by both prepayments and accruals at the same time. To get the exact cash paid, we start with the reported expense on the income statement. We then add any increase in prepaid expenses because paying in advance uses up our physical cash. We also subtract any increase in accrued expenses because unpaid bills mean we held onto our money. Doing both adjustments gives the exact cash outflow for that specific expense during the whole year.

    Example Computation: Payments for Rent

    Reported Rent Expense: 25,000
    Add: Increase in Prepaid Rent: 5,000
    Add: Decrease in Accrued Rent Payable: 3,000
    Cash Paid for Rent: 33,000
  5. Collections for Specific Income (Unearned & Accrued):
    Cash Collected = Income + Increase (or - Dec) Unearned - Increase (or + Dec) Accrued

    When computing for the exact cash collected from a specific income like consulting, we must check both unearned and accrued accounts. We start with the reported income amount listed on our current income statement. We then add any increase in unearned income because receiving advance payments brings in actual cash today. We also subtract any increase in accrued income because these are earned amounts that the customers have not yet paid. By combining these two adjustments, we find the true cash collections received for that specific income.

    Example Computation: Collections for Consulting

    Reported Consulting Income: 50,000
    Add: Increase in Unearned Consulting Income: 5,000
    Less: Increase in Accrued Consulting Income: (2,000)
    Cash Collected from Consulting: 53,000
  6. Interest and Taxes:
    Cash Paid = Interest/Tax Expense + Decrease (or - Inc) in Interest/Tax Payable

    Interest and taxes are reported separately to give users a very clear view of these major cash payments. We do not just use the expense amounts shown on the income statement because of timing differences. We must adjust the reported tax and interest expenses using their related payable accounts on the balance sheet. If the tax payable goes down, it means we paid extra cash to the government, so we add the decrease to the tax expense. This direct calculation accurately shows the heavy cash drain caused by government taxes and bank loans.

    Example Computation: Interest and Taxes Paid

    Interest Computation:
    Reported Interest Expense: 10,000
    Less: Increase in Interest Payable: (2,000)
    Cash Paid for Interest: 8,000
    Taxes Computation:
    Reported Income Tax Expense: 15,000
    Add: Decrease in Income Tax Payable: 3,000
    Cash Paid for Taxes: 18,000
