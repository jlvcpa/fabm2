Statement of Cash Flows (PAS 7)

"Welcome. Mastering the translation of accrual-based accounting into pure cash reality is paramount. This module guides you through PAS 7."

1. Overview and Purpose

Definition

The statement of cash flows summarizes cash and cash equivalents entering and leaving a company. It strips away non-cash transactions to reveal pure liquidity over a specific period.

Purpose & Role

  • Assesses the entity's ability to generate cash to pay obligations.
  • Reconciles accrual-based Net Income to actual cash flow.
  • Evaluates the financial structure (debt vs. equity).
  • Predicts future cash flows based on historical performance.

2. Cash and Cash Equivalents

The PAS 7 Criteria

  1. Short-term and highly liquid.
  2. Readily convertible to known amounts of cash.
  3. Subject to an insignificant risk of changes in value.

Core Rule: To qualify as a cash equivalent, an investment must have a maturity of three months or less from its acquisition date.

Examples of Classifications

  • 30-Day Treasury Bill: YES It is short-term (< 3 months), highly liquid, and low risk.
  • Investment in Equity Securities: NO Equity securities fail the criteria because they are subject to a significant risk of changes in value.
  • 1-Year Time Deposit: NO It fails the time-bound criteria since its maturity is greater than 3 months from the date of acquisition.

3. Classification of Cash Flows

Operating Activities

These are the principal revenue-producing activities of the entity. They generally result from the transactions and other events that enter into the determination of net income.

  • (+) Inflows: Sale of goods/services, royalties, fees, and trading securities sales.
  • (-) Outflows: Payments to suppliers, payments to employees, and trading securities purchases.

Investing Activities

These activities relate to the acquisition and disposal of long-term assets and other investments not included in cash equivalents.

  • (+) Inflows: Sale of Property, Plant, & Equipment (PPE), sale of debt/equity instruments of other entities.
  • (-) Outflows: Purchase of PPE, loans made to other parties.

Financing Activities

These activities result in changes in the size and composition of the contributed equity and borrowings (non-current liabilities and equity) of the entity.

  • (+) Inflows: Proceeds from issuing shares, proceeds from loans, notes, or bonds.
  • (-) Outflows: Repayments of borrowed loans, acquiring treasury stock, and cash dividends paid to owners.

4. Special Treatments (PAS 7)

Bank Overdrafts (Philippine Context)

Bank overdrafts are generally considered financing activities. However, if they are repayable on demand and form an integral part of an entity's cash management, they can be included as a component of cash equivalents. Note: In the Philippines, this is generally not permitted and is strictly classified as a current liability/financing activity.

Interest and Dividends

PAS 7 requires consistent classification from period to period:

  • Interest Paid: Operating or Financing
  • Interest Received: Operating or Investing
  • Dividends Paid: Financing or Operating
  • Dividends Received: Operating or Investing

Non-Cash Transactions

Investing and financing transactions that do not require the use of cash or cash equivalents are excluded from the body of the statement of cash flows. Instead, they must be disclosed in the notes to the financial statements. Examples include converting bonds to shares or acquiring assets via a lease.

5. Reporting Methods: Indirect Method

The Indirect Method adjusts accrual Net Income for non-cash effects, deferrals, and accruals. It is widely used in practice due to the ease of preparation directly from the existing Income Statement and comparative Statements of Financial Position.

Step-by-Step Procedure

  1. Start with Net Income: Sourced from the bottom line of the Income Statement.
  2. Add back Non-Cash Expenses: Since these expenses reduced Net Income but did not consume cash, they must be added back (e.g., Depreciation, Amortization, Impairment).
  3. Adjust for Non-Operating Gains/Losses: Deduct gains and add back losses from the sale of investing assets (like PPE). The actual full cash received from the sale will be reported in the Investing section, so we remove the accounting gain/loss from Operating to avoid double-counting.
  4. Adjust for Working Capital Changes: Analyze changes in Current Assets and Current Liabilities.
    • Current Assets: Increase = Deduct cash, Decrease = Add cash.
    • Current Liabilities: Increase = Add cash, Decrease = Deduct cash.

6. Reporting Methods: Direct Method

The Direct Method explicitly shows major classes of gross cash receipts and gross cash payments. It is recommended by PAS 7 because it provides more transparent cash flow insights and higher predictive value than the indirect method.

Step-by-Step Procedure

  1. Cash Received from Customers:
    Sales +/- Decrease/Increase in Accounts Receivable
  2. Cash Paid to Suppliers:
    COGS +/- Increase/Decrease in Inventory +/- Decrease/Increase in Accounts Payable
  3. Cash Paid for Operating Expenses:
    Operating Expenses (excluding non-cash items) +/- Increase/Decrease in Prepayments & Accruals
  4. Cash Paid for Interest and Taxes:
    Deduct cash directly paid during the period for these obligations.
